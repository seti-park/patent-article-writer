### Post 1

```text
The Tesla document published on 9 July at the international patent bureau is not about cars, batteries, or robots. It is about getting solar panels onto a roof. The machines sold to do that job today are dedicated track hoists at $3,500 and up, with a 4 HP gas engine at the entry price. The low end is still tying ropes around panels and pulling them by hand. Tesla's filing describes three add-on modules that turn a standard extension ladder and a jobsite drill into the hoist, with a claimed setup time of five minutes.

Tesla wants the version you assemble from what is already on the truck.
```

### Post 2

```text
The filing is WO 2026/148096 A1, "Ladder Hoist Assembly", and its whole mechanism fits in one glance: a carriage, a pulley, a gearbox, one rope. The carriage rides the ladder's front face and carries the load. The pulley sits at the top and turns the rope around. The gearbox hangs on the back face and winds or unwinds the rope to move the carriage. Front face is cargo, back face is power, and the ladder in between is the track.

The drill only arrives in the dependent claims and the specification. A drill spins fast and pulls weakly, and a hoist needs the opposite, so the gearbox is worm-driven. The specification's example is 10:1, every ten rotations of the drill becoming one rotation of the rope spool, the same trade a bicycle makes in its lowest gear. And nothing dangles. The load rides the carriage, the carriage is held to the ladder the whole way up, and the rope only has to pull. As drafted, claim 1 covers the bare arrangement, whatever spins the gearbox. The drill is not what Tesla is claiming. The arrangement is.
```

### Post 3

```text
The incumbents are dedicated machines. The TranzSporter TP250 ladder hoist lists at $3,500.99 with a 4 HP gas engine, and it ships with its own aluminum base track and ladder sections rather than clamping onto a ladder the crew already owns. Safety Hoist Company's solar-panel models, the SPH-250 and SPH-500, list at $5,040 and $5,710 and ride their own track sections. Those prices are market listings, not numbers from Tesla's ladder-hoist filing. The document's own words stop at existing assemblies being "complicated and costly to setup or install". Tesla's counter-pitch is setup "within five minutes" against existing solutions that "may require over half an hour". No load rating is published, so the honest comparison is product shape: a machine you buy whole versus a kit you clamp onto a ladder you already own. The filing's second independent claim makes that shape explicit. It claims a kit for converting an extension ladder, and unlike claim 1 its gearbox comes "including a hand drill adapter". One claim seeks the geometry, no matter what drives it. The other seeks the exact box a crew would buy.
```
