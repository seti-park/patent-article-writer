# Tesla's New Filing Turns a Ladder and a Drill Into a Solar Hoist

![FIG. 1A: Tesla's ladder hoist assembly installed on a jobsite extension ladder.](input/figures/fig-01A.png)

*FIG. 1A: the first drawing in the filing is a photograph, not a CAD render. The claimed arrangement sits on a standard extension ladder against a residential roofline: carriage (102) riding the front face, gearbox (104) at the back, rope (180) running up the ladder (150).*

## A Hoist Made From What Is Already on the Truck

The Tesla document published on 9 July at the international patent bureau is not about cars, batteries, or robots. It is three add-on modules that turn a standard extension ladder and a jobsite drill into a solar-panel hoist [0037], with a claimed setup time of five minutes [0038]. The machines sold to do that job today are dedicated track hoists at $3,500 and up, with a 4 HP gas engine at the entry price. Tesla wants the version you assemble from what is already on the truck.

The document is WO 2026/148096 A1, "Ladder Hoist Assembly", and two things are true about it at once. It is an application, granted nowhere, so every claim in it is a request and every number in it is Tesla's own assertion. And it is the rare Tesla filing whose whole mechanism fits in one glance: a carriage, a pulley, a gearbox, one rope [0024]. Arranged the way the claims describe, they ask equipment a roofing crew already owns to do the job those dedicated machines are sold for.

## Claim 1 Claims the Arrangement, Not the Drill

Strip the filing to its first claim and what remains is geometry. A carriage rides the ladder's front face and carries the load: in the claim's words, "a carriage movably attached to a first major side of a ladder, the carriage configured to carry the materials" [0005]. "A pulley attached to a top portion of the ladder" turns the rope around [0005]. The power unit hangs on the opposite face:

> "a gearbox attached to a second major side of the ladder that is opposite to the first major side of the ladder, the gearbox connected to the carriage through a rope routing at least partially on the pulley"
> WO 2026/148096 A1, [0005]

*FIG. 1B: the pulley as installed in the field. One bracket hooks over the top rung (154), a second locks at the rung below (156), and the rope (180) rides the wheel between them.*

The gearbox's one claimed job is to "wind or unwind the rope to move the carriage along the first major side of the ladder" [0005]. The rope's route is the whole trick: from the gearbox on the back, up over the pulley at the top, then down the front to the carriage [0041]. Front face is cargo, back face is power, and the ladder in between is the track.

Everything a casual reader would guess is the invention sits outside that claim. The drill arrives in dependent claims and the specification, where the gearbox "transfers a torque from a drill to rotate the spool to wind or unwind the rope" [0020]. The worm-gear ratios are a menu of dependent-claim options [0019]. The side rollers that wrap the ladder's rails [0044], the remote control [0022], the material tray [0023]: options, all of them. As drafted, claim 1 covers the bare arrangement, whatever spins the gearbox. **The drill is not what Tesla is claiming. The arrangement is.**

*FIG. 2: the whole kit on one sheet. Left to right: the gearbox (104) with rung bracket (232) and drill adapter (234), the carriage (102), and the pulley (106).*

## The Gearbox Trades Drill Speed for Winch Torque

A hand drill does not sound like a machine that hauls solar panels to a roofline. The cheapest incumbent answer to this job is a 4 HP gas engine, and the doubt is first-order physics: a drill spins fast and pulls weakly, and a hoist needs the opposite.

The filing's answer is gear reduction, and it is specific. The gearbox is worm-driven, and the specification's example is "a 10:1 worm-driven gearbox in which every ten rotations of the drill result in one rotation of the spool" [0045]. Ten turns in become one slower turn out, the same trade a bicycle makes in its lowest gear. A dependent claim keeps the menu open at 5:1, 10:1, and 15:1 [0019]. The drill's chuck grips an adapter on the gearbox's input shaft [0055], the worm turns the spool, and the spool winds the rope [0020].

The other half of the answer is that nothing dangles. The load rides the carriage, and the carriage is held to the ladder the whole way up, its side rollers wrapped around the ladder's rails [0044]. The rope only has to pull. The ladder does the steadying.

*FIG. 4: the gearbox (104) opened up. The rung bracket (232) hooks a rung, the drill adapter (234) takes the drill's chuck, and the spool (402) winds the rope.*

## The Market Sells This Job as a $3,500 Machine

The incumbents are dedicated machines. The TranzSporter TP250 ladder hoist lists at $3,500.99 with a 4 HP Lifan gas engine, rising to $4,189.99 for the electric version. It does not clamp onto a ladder the crew already owns. It ships with its own aluminum base track and three 8-foot ladder sections. Safety Hoist Company's solar-panel models, the SPH-250 and SPH-500, list at $5,040 and $5,710. They run on 110 V electric motors, ride their own track sections, and carry the vendor's own setup claim of "less than 15 minutes."

That is the world the filing writes against: existing roof-lifting assemblies "utilize custom ladders, batteries, and/or motors such that the assemblies can be complicated and costly to setup or install" [0039], while the low-end alternative is still "tying ropes around panels and manually pulling the ropes" [0039]. Tesla's counter-pitch is that its assembly "leverages standard, readily available, and/or off-the-shelf components" [0041], and that:

> "the ladder hoist assembly can be readily available and/or setup in much less time (e.g., within five minutes) compared with existing solutions that may require over half an hour to setup"
> WO 2026/148096 A1, [0038]

Both of those setup numbers are the filing's assertions, hedged in the original with "e.g." and "may", and the text publishes no load rating to set against the incumbents' specifications. The honest comparison is not performance, which is unmeasured. It is product shape: a machine you buy whole versus a kit you clamp onto a ladder you already own.

The filing's second independent claim makes that shape explicit. It claims "a kit for converting an extension ladder to use for transporting materials up to a roof" [0024], and unlike claim 1, the kit does commit to the drill. Its gearbox comes "including a hand drill adapter" [0024], its ladder must have "a base section and at least one fly section" [0024], and turning that adapter is what slides the carriage up the rails [0024]. The device claim stays broad and the kit claim is the tool-in-the-box version. One seeks the geometry, no matter what drives it. The other seeks the exact box a crew would buy.

## The Target Is the Crew's Time, Not the Panel

Why does a company that stopped publishing its solar installation numbers after 2023 care about ladder accessories? The answer is a reading of the market, not a stated motive: the cost problem in US residential solar has moved off the hardware and into the hours. Installations fell in 2025 and are projected to fall roughly another 18 percent in 2026. Tesla now positions its solar business around selling panels through third-party installers. NREL benchmarking, as reported by trade press, puts non-hardware soft costs, installation labor among them, at roughly half or more of a US residential system's cost.

The filing fits an install-time story Tesla is already telling in public. On 30 January 2026 the company launched a US-made 420 W solar panel with an integrated rail-less mounting system, publicly claiming it cuts installation time by more than 30 percent. A panel that mounts faster and a hoist that rigs in a claimed five minutes [0038] attack the same line item: crew hours on site. The filing names its cargo accordingly, "moving photovoltaic panels upward to the roofs of residential homes" [0037]. The names on the cover fit too: per their public LinkedIn profiles, two of the four inventors are Tesla mechanical engineers on the energy side, one of them on a team called Solar Tools.

The second embodiment pushes the same logic one step further. The gearbox "can be remotely controlled" [0058], so the operator can stand clear while the load moves, which the filing frames as "enhancing safety by reducing exposure to items or loads moving along the ladder" [0060]. A tray on the carriage forms a slot that holds long stock like conduit, so it "cannot roll off, slide laterally, and/or pivot unexpectedly" on the way up [0063]. Both are options in dependent claims [0022] [0023], not requirements of the arrangement.

*FIG. 8A: the second embodiment in use. Conduit (842) rides through the tray's slot (634) while the remote-controllable gearbox (604) drives the rope (180) up the ladder.*

## A Guided Carriage Is Not a Hung Load

The strongest objection to all of this is age. A winch mounted on an extension ladder was patented in 1978 by an individual inventor: US 4,128,228 A, "Extension ladder hoist". That machine put a winch on a ladder rung and drove it with an AC motor or a hand crank. Its load hung from a retention hook on the cable. Drill-powered winches are commodity retail items today, and hobbyists have improvised drill-and-rope ladder lifts for decades. Concede all of it: no single ingredient here is new.

The two documents part ways at the load. The 1978 hoist dangles its cargo from the cable, and the ladder is only a mast. Claim 1 puts the cargo on a carriage "movably attached to a first major side" of the ladder [0005] and keeps it captive to that face the whole way up. Optional hardware in the dependent claims does the guiding: side rollers wrap the ladder's rails so the carriage cannot slip off sideways [0044]. Ramped sliding plates carry it over the step where the base and fly sections overlap, "without getting stuck or encountering uneven resistance" [0051].

That overlap ramp is the detail that gives the game away, because a load swinging on a cable never needs one. The filing's engineering concentrates exactly where a hung load offers nothing to engineer: keeping the cargo captive to the ladder's face. The ingredients are old, and the incumbents still bring custom ladders of their own [0039]: today's machines ride a dedicated base track, not the crew's ladder. What claim 1 asks for is the thing the 1978 document never drew, a carriage attached to the ladder's face rather than a load hung from a cable.

*FIG. 3: the carriage (102) in detail. Ramped sliding plates (202) let it ride over the ladder's section overlap, and side rollers (208) fold out to wrap the rails.*

## All of It Is Still an Application

What Tesla holds today is a publication, not a patent. The application was filed on 31 December 2025 and claims priority to a provisional from 3 January 2025, the placeholder filing that reserved that earlier date. It published on 9 July 2026, on the ordinary eighteen-month clock that runs from a priority date to international publication. The published record shows no examination, and it grants nothing yet.

The published text also contains no international search report and cites no prior art. So the baseline an examiner will hold claim 1 against, whether the 1978 patent or something closer, is not yet on the record. Every scope statement above is therefore sought scope: what Tesla is asking for, not what it owns. One further caveat is mechanical. The claims in the public copy are a garbled scan, so the citations here point to the part of the filing that spells each claim out again in intact text. The independent claims are unaffected: device claim 1 and kit claim 20 are clean boundaries.

## The Next Document to Read Is the Examiner's

The verdict on the filing reads straight off its parts list. This is Tesla engineering pointed at the least glamorous line in solar's cost structure. What it seeks is not a new machine but the arrangement that turns a standard ladder and a standard drill into the incumbents' $3,500 job. One warning belongs beside that, specific to this document: its drawings photograph the assembly installed on a real jobsite ladder. A photographed assembly in a patent application is still a drawing, not a product plan. The limits in the previous section apply to everything else.

The watch event is already on the calendar. The international search report will show what an examiner stacks between claim 1 and a grant. The national-phase record, the filings Tesla makes in individual patent offices, will show whether the guided carriage holds up as the distinguishing arrangement. Until then the score is simple to state. The incumbents sell the machine. Tesla is asking to own the conversion.
