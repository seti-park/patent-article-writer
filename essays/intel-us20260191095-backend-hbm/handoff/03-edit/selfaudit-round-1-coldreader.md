# Cold Reader — Round 1

*Casual scroller. Read once at normal pace, no checklist. Three things only.*

## 1. Where did I stop or start skimming?

I started skimming here, in the "A Tower Built to Match HBM4's Footprint" section:

> "Traffic moves down the stack through what the patent calls TSV gutters: columns of
> through-silicon vias, the vertical wires punched straight through a die [0073]. Four
> gutters are cut into every die, each carrying the data for a pair of sub-channels [0034]."

That's the spot my eyes glazed. Up to there I was reading every word. Right there it turned
into an engineering spec sheet — TSV gutters, through-silicon vias, four gutters, sub-channels,
then UCIe right after — and I stopped caring about the exact parts and just scrolled for the
"so what." The part-number figure captions (151, 153 / 161A-161H / 162 to 165) I skipped
entirely.

I re-engaged one section later at "the leap is mine, not the document's" and read closely
again to the end, because that's where it finally told me *why I should care* (who gets to
make the memory).

## 2. What did I feel?

Hooked at the top. "A Memory Architecture From the Company That Left It" plus "Intel sells
none of it" is a genuinely good pull — I wanted to know why Intel of all companies filed this.
Then bored/glazed through the middle tower section, which felt like it was showing its
homework. Then re-hooked and honestly a little impressed by the fab-bottleneck argument and
the "backend is the one word that changes who can build it" idea — that's the part that made
the whole thing feel like it mattered to money and not just to engineers. Low-grade acronym
fatigue throughout (HBM4, TSV, UCIe, 1T1C, ZAM, HB3DM, imec, VLSI stacked up). The bear-case
section near the end felt fair rather than salesy, which made me trust it more.

## 3. The one thing I'd repeat to a friend

"Intel filed a patent that builds the memory cell up in the chip's wiring instead of the
silicon base — which, if the yields work out, could let a foundry like TSMC make the AI
memory everyone's short on and break the three-company stranglehold. But it's just a filing
with no numbers yet; the real test is later this year."
