# Title-Lead Candidates

Five title + lead-¶1 pairs, one per energy register, all riding the locked spine's facts (a register changes delivery order and polarity, never the evidence). Titles ≤ 70 characters, no em-dash. Leads are beat-first: no verdict-insurance fact (pending status, "not a product") ahead of the register's beat. Posture is `measured` — the lead delivers the finding, then prices it; it never opens defensively.

## discovery

- **title**: Intel's Patent Moves the DRAM Cell Into the Back-End
- **lead**: Intel's newest memory filing does something quietly radical: it builds the DRAM cell's transistor in the chip's back-end wiring layers, not in the crystalline-silicon front-end where DRAM has always lived. Stack those dies eight high, the application says, to match HBM4's footprint. Move the cell to the back-end and you change something bigger than a spec: you change where this kind of memory could be made.
- **rationale**: Delivers the reader_sentence's core finding (the cell moves into back-end layers → "who can make HBM" changes) as ¶1's payoff, invention-first, with the pricing held for later — the natural fit for a measured verdict.

## tension

- **title**: Intel Quit Merchant Memory in 2021. Now It Filed This
- **lead**: Intel sold its memory business in 2021 and wrote off Optane the next year. Yet a patent it published in July 2026 architects HBM-class memory all the way down to the DRAM cell, and builds that cell in the chip's back-end layers. Both facts are real. The filing is where "Intel is out of memory" and "Intel is designing memory" stop contradicting each other.
- **rationale**: Collides Intel-exited-memory with Intel-designing-memory (the natural mate of the corporate-narrative-friction hook); the reader_sentence resolves the collision as an architectural move, not a business re-entry.

## contrarian

- **title**: No, Intel's "Ultra HBM" Patent Isn't the ZAM Story
- **lead**: Read the headlines and Intel's "Ultra HBM" looks like the ZAM "HBM killer" doing the rounds. The filing tells a different story: where ZAM stacks on a diagonal Z-angle with hybrid bonding, this one uses vertical TSV gutters and UCIe, and its real move is putting the 1T1C DRAM cell in the back-end. Same direction, different document, and a bigger implication than "Intel builds an HBM rival."
- **rationale**: States the real, citable consensus (the press conflates this with ZAM / "Intel back in memory"), then inverts it with the filing — the reader leaves able to correct the crowd, carrying the reader_sentence.

## insider

- **title**: Reading the Intel Patent Behind the "Ultra HBM" Headlines
- **lead**: Almost nobody repeating the "Intel HBM" headlines has opened the document underneath them. US 2026/0191095 A1 is that document, and it is more specific than the coverage: a 1T1C DRAM cell built from back-end transistors, stacked eight high over a UCIe base die, aimed at HBM4's footprint. Here is what it actually commits to, and what it pointedly does not say.
- **rationale**: Privileged-view framing — the reader sees the primary document, through the essayist; the reader_sentence becomes something they read for themselves, not a claim they were handed.

## stakes

- **title**: Three Companies Make HBM. Intel Filed a Fourth Path
- **lead**: Three companies make the world's high-bandwidth memory, and one of them, SK hynix, holds roughly 60% of it. Intel's new patent aims at that concentration from an unexpected angle: build the DRAM cell in the chip's back-end layers, where a logic-and-packaging line, not only a dedicated DRAM fab, could in principle carry it. If that works, the shortlist of who can make HBM gets longer.
- **rationale**: Leads with the concentration stake the investor already feels (a three-player club, ~60% one vendor); the reader_sentence prices what a fourth path would buy.
