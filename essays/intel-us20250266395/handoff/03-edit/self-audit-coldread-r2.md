# Cold Read — Round 2 (casual scroller, single read)

## (a) THE STOP POINT

I read to the end. The thing that kept pulling me was the plain-English translation of a
dry patent — it kept telling me *why I should care* right when I was about to drift.

But if I'm being honest about where I nearly bailed, it was here:

> "In the cavity embodiments, the tested assembly is lowered onto a substrate whose top surface holds a cavity shaped for the bridge, and the description says what the through-bridge path is for: it 'is often used to enable power to be routed into the bridge component' from 'a source located at a bottom of a cavity in a substrate' [0035]."

That whole "Power Comes Up Through the Floor" section is where it got thick — cavities,
FIG. 7, FIG. 8, FIG. 9, FIG. 10, through-glass vias, embodiments — a lot of figure numbers
and jargon stacked up fast, and my eyes started sliding. I skimmed the middle of that
section.

The last sentence that kept me going long enough to finish:

> "The paperwork moved first last time."

That's a good closer. Punchy, and it made the whole "watch the documents, not the hype"
point land.

## (b) HOW IT FELT

The opening grabbed me. "Intel Filed the Packaging Flow That Comes After EMIB-T" plus that
first punchy idea — "The technology in the news is the bridge getting power vias. The filing
is the bridge changing sides." — that's a clean hook. I got what the whole thing was about
in the first two paragraphs, which almost never happens with patent stuff. I leaned in.

The known-good-die math section was the best part: "At 95 percent per die, a four-die
package comes out about 81 percent good, and a twenty-die package about 35 percent." That's
a number I can feel. That's where I actually understood why this matters and why it's about
money, not engineering trivia.

Where it dragged: the middle, the "Power Comes Up Through the Floor" stretch. Too many
FIG. this and FIG. that, too many parts numbers in parentheses (526, 536, 701, 702, 704,
872, 882, 860). It felt like the writer got excited about the drawings and forgot I can't
see them the way they can. I skimmed.

Did it talk down to me? No — actually the opposite, it trusted me maybe a touch too much in
that middle patch. The glossing ("A micron is a millionth of a meter") was nice and never
felt condescending.

The ending recovered strongly. The "concede all of that, look at what's left" turn was
satisfying — it argued against itself and then landed the point, which made me trust it.

## (c) THE ONE THING I'D REPEAT

"Intel quietly filed a chip-packaging method that tests the expensive part before building
it — bond the chips to the little bridge first, test the whole cluster, and only then commit
a board — and they filed it more than a year before the flashy 'EMIB-T' version they showed
off on stage."
