# Cold read — casual X scroller (round 3)

Read: handoff/03-edit/essay-final.md, top to bottom, once. Header image (FIG. 5) viewed; body figures appeared as captions.

## 1. Where I stopped or skimmed

I read the whole thing, but I skimmed three times:

- The long legal quote in the memory section — "Another embodiment in this disclosure is a package that includes an IC comprising a systolic array of data processing units (DPUs) and a separate memory device comprising a plurality of channels..." My eyes slid straight off it. The plain-English translation right above it had already done the job, so the quote felt like homework I was allowed to skip.
- The back half of "Math That Forgets, a Sidecar That Remembers", starting around "One attention step alone, computing what the filing calls query values, means the data 'passes through the systolic array three times'". Second deep-technical stretch in a row; I wanted to get back to the company story and skimmed to the section break. This is where the article dragged for me.
- The bracketed registry codes in the loan section — "(USPTO reel/frame 067204/0877)" — pure noise to me; I hopped over them, though the loan story around them I read closely.

I never actually stopped. The money/rejection section pulled me back in.

## 2. What I felt, section by section

- Title + header image: receipts energy. The diagram itself is dry, but the caption ("pitched in June 2026... the drawing is dated May 2023") is what sold the click.
- The Pitch Has a Paper Trail: hooked; dates-as-plot is easy to follow. "The rest of the record prices exactly that" was a bit finance-speak for me.
- One Giant Array: easier than expected — the lego-brick picture (one small chip, made many times, acting as one huge grid) just works. Small "did I miss episode 1?" bump at the mention of a companion analysis of some other patent.
- The Memory Claims Delete the Switch: this is where I felt smartest — the moment I got WHY you can delete the switch: the AI model's weights never move and always go to the same columns, so every column can be hardwired to its own memory channel and the middleman is dead weight. The pitch line "the best layer is no layer" suddenly meant something concrete. (Runner-up smart moment: learning an RCE is basically a fee to keep arguing after the examiner says no.)
- Math That Forgets, a Sidecar That Remembers: great title, and "a machine with one job, repeated forever" landed. Then it got dense (attention passes, layer normalization, 98% utilization) and I sagged — though "this one budgeted its idle cycles" was a nice recovery line.
- Etched Keeps Paying to Own It: the juiciest section. Rejected in October, paid again in April; patents pledged as loan collateral, twice; "The founders wrote it down early. The patent office decides whether early also meant original." Felt like real dirt with no hype in either direction.
- An Asset in Formation: satisfying, fair ending. "In the pitch the layer is already gone. At the patent office, Etched is still paying to own the deletion." — that's the line I'll remember.

## 3. What I'd repeat to a friend tomorrow

"You know that 'best layer is no layer' memory thing the Etched guys keep pitching? Turns out the founders literally wrote it into their first patent filing in May 2023, three years before the hype thread — but the patent office rejected it and they're still paying to fight for it. So the idea is genuinely theirs; they just don't own it yet."

## 4. Would I have tapped from the feed?

Yes — but only because of the date. "No-Switch Memory Idea" means nothing to me from a feed, and the first image is a dry patent diagram; what earns the tap is "Put It in Writing in May 2023" landing in the middle of people arguing whether Etched is real or vapor — it promises receipts, and receipts are exactly what that argument was missing.
