# Cold read — casual X scroller (blind, round 2)

Reader: not a patent person, not an investor, not an engineer. Clicked because people were
arguing about Etched on the timeline. Read `handoff/03-edit/essay-final.md` once, top to
bottom, at reading speed, with the figures the essay shows.

## 1. Where I stopped or skimmed

I read it all — the argument-receipts angle kept me going — but I skimmed in four places,
two small and two real.

- First wobble (small): "A systolic array, the structure being multiplied here, is a grid
  of small arithmetic units that pass data to their neighbors on every tick" — this
  paragraph felt like class starting. "Tensor" went by un-glossed and I just accepted
  "numbers stream in from the left" and moved on.
- Skipped mid-quote (small): the long block quote starting "Another embodiment in this
  disclosure is a package that includes an IC comprising a systolic array of data
  processing units (DPUs)..." — the essay had just translated it for me one paragraph
  earlier, so I bailed on the legalese two lines in.
- Biggest drag: second half of "Math That Forgets, a Sidecar That Remembers". Attention
  genuinely dropped at "One attention step alone, computing the queries, means the data
  'passes through the systolic array three times' [0055]." By then I had the idea (one
  machine, one job, side-brain for memory) and this felt like a second helping of
  engineering when I could smell the money section coming. I scrolled faster through the
  98%-utilization / layer-normalization bit.
- Second drag: "One more registry note, offered as an observation." The PCT /
  continuation / family-tree paragraph read like homework for patent nerds. Short, so I
  got through it, but eyes glazed. Same for the "(USPTO reel/frame 067204/0877)" codes —
  I slid right over those.

## 2. What I felt, section by section

- Title + header image: curious. "In writing in May 2023" is receipts energy. The diagram
  itself is dry; the caption did the saving.
- The Pitch Has a Paper Trail: hooked. Hype numbers vs. a dated public document is exactly
  the ammo I clicked for. "Verdict in miniature" felt a bit writerly but fine.
- One Giant Array, Stitched From Identical Chips: mostly easy. "One small chip design,
  manufactured many times" clicked instantly — lego into one giant chip. The systolic-array
  explainer bit was the one classroom moment.
- The Memory Claims Delete the Switch: best section. "That switch is the layer this filing
  exists to delete" — got it immediately. The bolded line about claim language dated May
  2023 is the payoff of the whole piece.
- Math That Forgets, a Sidecar That Remembers: great title, cool floor-plan idea
  (forgetting math vs. remembering sidecar), but the scheduling half was the slog — see
  above.
- Etched Keeps Paying to Own It: spiciest section. Wait, it got REJECTED in October 2025?
  Nobody arguing in my feed mentioned that. Loans secured against the patents, lien three
  days after two grants — that's the gossip-grade material. The reel/frame numbers are
  noise to me.
- An Asset in Formation: satisfying. "At the patent office, Etched is still paying to own
  the deletion" is a genuinely good last line.

The one moment I felt smartest: realizing "the best layer is no layer" is not a slogan but
a literal missing component — I could look back at the header drawing, see wires going
straight from memory to columns with nothing in between, and then the essay handed me "The
pitch and the claims name the same absence, and this document is where the absence has a
date." I felt like I caught the trick myself. (Close second: learning an RCE is "a fee
paid to keep arguing after the examiner has said no with finality" — I now understand a
real patent-office mechanic.)

## 3. What I'd repeat to a friend tomorrow

"You know that Etched chip company everyone's fighting about? Their whole 'best memory
layer is no layer' pitch was literally written into their first patent in May 2023, three
years before the hype thread — but the patent office has already rejected it once and
they're still paying to argue, and their loans are backed by those same patents."

## 4. Would I have tapped from the feed?

Yes — but only because Etched was already drama in my feed; the title's dated-receipts
promise ("Put It in Writing in May 2023") is what sells the tap, since the first image is
a gray patent diagram I'd normally scroll straight past.
