# OpenAI's Chip Patent Does Floating-Point Math on Integer Adders

![FIG. 4, mantissa-alignment and column adder-tree core](input/figures/fig-04.png)

FIG. 4: the align-once core as a functional block diagram [0138]. After multiplication, every product is shifted onto one shared scale, then an adder tree sums the aligned results as plain integers [0011]. This is claim 1's align-then-integer-accumulate step, drawn as data flow.

## The Design Was on File Before It Was News

There is a published OpenAI patent that designs an AI chip down to the individual memory cell. The math runs inside the memory array itself [0133], and the expensive part, adding up a block of floating-point products, is done by plain integer adders after one clever shift [0011]. The document is US 2026/0147536 A1, "Alignment in Hardware Accelerators," and its priority date is 22 November 2024. OpenAI and Broadcom announced ten gigawatts of "OpenAI-designed AI accelerators" on 13 October 2025, about eleven months later (see Sources, Official statements).

Eleven months before OpenAI announced its own chip, the math engine was already on file, written to the bitcell, the circuit that stores a single bit. That is the call this record supports, and it has two sides. The filing is primary evidence that "OpenAI-designed" is literal, transistor-level engineering rather than press framing. It is also a pending application rather than a granted patent, which prices the evidence without changing what it shows.

## Multiply in Floating Point, Add as Integers, Convert Once

The engine is a compute-in-memory macro: a block of memory that does arithmetic where the data already sits, instead of shuttling every number to a separate processor, an architecture the document credits with "reducing the von Neumann bottleneck" [0133]. The data path reads in one pass. Activations, the live inputs of a neural-network layer, stream in through an input buffer [0142]. Weights, the learned numbers, stay put: they are the stationary operand, stored in the macro's memory cells [0130]. Finished dot products leave through a single conversion step at the output [0145].

*FIG. 1: the one-pass datapath. Activations stream in through an input buffer; the weights sit in the 32×32 CIM macro (101); a mode decoding unit (110) sets the numeric format [0006]; the dequantization unit (106) converts to FP22 once [0142] [0145].*

The formats involved are tiny. FP8, FP6, and FP4 are floating-point numbers squeezed into 8, 6, and 4 bits, and the patent's premise is that shrinking numbers this way speeds up deep learning with minimal accuracy loss [0131]. The cost sits elsewhere. Conventional accelerator pipelines keep transforming values between numeric domains, and every transformation is hardware [0005]:

> "Each conversion step requires additional scaling, rounding, and saturation logic, which adds area, power, and latency to already dense compute fabrics, and can introduce cumulative quantization errors that degrade numerical behavior over many layers."
> US 2026/0147536 A1, [0005]

What independent claims 1, 20, and 29 seek to protect is a structure that pays that cost once. A floating-point number keeps its digits and its scale apart: the mantissa carries the digits, the exponent says where the point sits. In plain terms, each column cell multiplies one activation by one stored weight and emits a small floating-point result the document calls a primitive product [0011]. Then, in the claim's own wording:

> "a functional block configured to align mantissa bits of primitive products generated by column cells in the computing unit by shifting the mantissa bits"
> "an adder tree configured to output an accumulation value of the primitive products in an integer format by adding the shifted mantissa bits"
> US 2026/0147536 A1, [0011]

*FIG. 3: one column cell, where a single primitive product is made [0011].*

The alignment is the move you already know from adding decimals by hand: line up the points first, then add the digits, then place the point once in the answer. Here the lining up is a bit shift. The macro finds the largest exponent among the 32 products in a block and computes how far each mantissa must shift to sit on that shared scale [0199] [0200]. That puts every contribution on a consistent footing before the sum [0104].

The numbers stay small by design. What the adder tree adds up is an ordinary integer, and only a tiny exponent rides along to track the scale. For an FP8 activation times an FP6 weight, the largest exponent any product can carry is 22, which fits in a 5-bit integer [0199]. After the shift, the adder tree sums the column and hands out a 35-bit integer plus that 5-bit exponent [0145]. A dequantization unit then folds the block's shared scale factor back in and converts the result to FP22, a wider floating-point format, in one step [0141] [0145].

The point of the shuffle is what the adder tree is made of: a synthesizable integer adder tree rather than floating-point hardware [0139], so the expensive half of the matrix math runs on the cheapest kind of adder a chip can synthesize [0006] [0011]. **Align once, and the whole accumulation becomes integer arithmetic.** One alignment, one integer sum, one conversion back: that arithmetic is block floating point [0141], a shared-scale style older than this filing, so the trick itself is not OpenAI's invention. What the claim seeks to own is the compute-in-memory circuit that executes it inside the array [0011] [0133].

## The Headline Features Are Described. The Circuits Are Claimed

The description promises more than the claims do, and the difference matters. In the description, one physical datapath serves FP8, FP6, or FP4 activations against FP6 or FP4 weights [0130], steered by a mode decoding unit, "without requiring separate conversion pipelines or dedicated cores for each format" [0006]. And the data layout underneath, blocks of 32 values sharing one scaling factor [0141], is not OpenAI's idea at all. It is the shape the industry standardized as OCP MX in September 2023, signed by AMD, Arm, Intel, Meta, Microsoft, Nvidia, and Qualcomm (see Sources, Technical specs).

So the concession comes first. The format agility that leads the document's pitch is description material: independent claims 1, 20, and 29 seek the multiply-align-add triad, not the mode decoding unit and not the format switching [0011]. And block-scaled arithmetic is established practice, standardized a year before this filing's priority date. A skeptic can fairly call the core a known technique, implemented.

The claim set answers with specificity rather than breadth. The named machinery is pulled up into the independent claims themselves. Claim 20 folds in a shift calculation and select decoding unit that works out each product's shift [0200], and claim 29 pulls in a shift register, each carried alongside the same multiply-align-add triad. The dependent claims add the specifics: the shift register sized by rule to the maximum exponent plus the mantissa bit count [0202], and a logarithmic multiplexer tree that does the shifting in stages [0206]. The description also counts what such choices buy: one optimization in the exponent path saves 64 by 64 half adders and full adders across the example 32-by-32 array [0183].

That is the distinction the skeptical read misses. MX is a data format, a public agreement about what the numbers look like. What this filing claims is machinery that executes those blocks natively inside a memory macro, and the more universal the format becomes, the more that machinery is worth. Wherever the claim boundaries settle, the document remains a dated, assigned record of that machinery worked out at circuit level, and the thesis rests on the record.

## The Design Target Is a Result Every Cycle

All of that machinery serves one performance promise:

> "the CIM macro 101 operates in a pipelined fashion, returning a VMM product (i.e., a vector of dot products) every cycle"
> US 2026/0147536 A1, [0147]

A VMM is the document's shorthand for vector-matrix multiplication, the operation that dominates AI training and inference [0004]. Once the pipeline is full, a complete vector of dot products drops out at every clock tick [0147].

Feeding that pipeline is the second half of the design. Weights change rarely: in the description's example, a single set of weight values serves 256 VMM operations before it needs replacing [0154]. For the replacement itself, the document describes memory cells built from two bitcells each, so the next weight matrix loads into one bank while the other bank feeds the ongoing computation [0012]. It describes this in its summary passages, not in the claims it is seeking. The stated aim is to "minimize compute stalls during updates" [0015].

*FIG. 43: the double-bitcell cell (4303). BC0 and BC1 share bit lines but have separate write-wordlines, so one bank loads the next weights while the other feeds the running computation [0012] [0015].*

The document is disciplined about its own superlative, and the hedge deserves to be kept: "In some embodiments, 100% utilization of the compute engine 100 may be possible." [0154] These are design statements by an applicant, not measurements of silicon. What they establish is intent: an engine whose adders are not supposed to idle, either for format conversions [0005] or for weight reloads [0154].

## A Pending Application, Written at Tape-Out Depth

The registry facts, stated once. US 2026/0147536 A1 was published on 28 May 2026, from an application filed on 21 November 2025, claiming priority to a provisional filed on 22 November 2024. The applicant and assignee is OpenAI Opco, LLC. It is a pending application: the claims can still be amended or rejected in examination, and nothing in it is yet an enforceable right. The publication also marks claims 11 through 19 as "(canceled)". The document gives no reason, and none is guessed at here.

Five inventors are named. One name carries press-reported history: Bloomberg reported in June 2024 that Jean-Didier Allegrucci, after roughly seventeen years leading SoC engineering at Apple, had been hired as head of hardware by Rain AI, a compute-in-memory chip startup (see Sources, News & media). That stays labeled as background, with its bound stated plainly: no document here says where any of the five sat on the provisional date.

What the record does establish is depth. The specification descends to latch-level bitcell circuits, non-overlapping clock generation, setup-time relations, and timing checks across process, voltage, and temperature corners [0234] [0262] [0289]. It even has the same memory cells doing double duty as test structures, wired to "serve both compute storage and scan-test roles" [0118]. Concept filings rarely carry clock margins and scan modes. Documents written toward manufacture do, and this one reads like engineering rather than positioning.

## Literal Engineering, With Two Dates to Watch

"OpenAI-designed" is literal engineering, and this filing is the primary evidence: a compute-in-memory math engine specified down to the individual memory cell, carrying an OpenAI-assigned priority date about eleven months older than the announcement that made the phrase famous. The announcement is from October 2025. The engineering is dated November 2024. The bounds priced in the previous section scope that call, and they do not reverse it.

One guard belongs to this patent specifically. Nothing connects these circuits to Jalapeño, the inference chip OpenAI and Broadcom unveiled in June 2026, and no source shows this macro running in silicon (see Sources, Official statements).

Two dates will grade the call. The first is the end of 2026, Jalapeño's company-claimed initial deployment window, where the announcement's schedule meets reality. The second belongs to the patent office: what examination leaves of claims 1, 20, and 29 will fix how much of the align-once, integer-accumulate engine OpenAI ends up owning. The chip story was told in 2025. The design record says the work started earlier, and the record is the harder document to argue with.
