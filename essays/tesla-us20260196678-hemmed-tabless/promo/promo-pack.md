## 2. English thread (2-4 message-unit posts)

```text
Tesla made tabless famous at Battery Day 2020, as a manufacturing-simplification story. On today's tabless lines, that simplification still involves cutting: lasers slit the bare foil edge into flags before winding, and equipment vendors sell 46-series laser notching and winding machines as a product category for those lines. Samsung SDI has announced 46-series cylindrical cells on a tabless design. The category is no longer Tesla-only.

Tesla's next tabless filing doesn't cut the foil into flags. It folds the edge, and the fold is the tab.
```

```text
The mechanism is a hem, like the hem of a trouser leg. The electrode film sits on a foil that keeps a bare edge, and that edge is folded back on itself until it extends past the film. Wound into a jellyroll, one winding of the hemmed edge touches the next, so the finished cell carries a continuous folded rim at one end. The collector attaches to that rim as both electrical contact and mechanical attachment. No tab is welded on. Nothing is cut away. The fold does the tab's job, and the cell stays in the tabless category. What changes is the shape of the terminal: the whole edge, not a local tongue welded at one spot.
```

```text
Fold instead of cut, and a whole list of machines may have no job left. The filing names the equipment a hemmed line "may not necessarily require": a tabbing device, slitting device, notching device, flag interleaving device, or debris handling device, plus the exhaust systems for all of them. Neither add a tab nor subtract a flag. Only fold.

The performance logic runs through the same geometry. The document's argument is that collecting current along the entire folded rim, instead of through one welded tab, shortens how far electrons travel between the cell terminals and the active material, which is what lets it claim improved internal electrical resistance.
```
